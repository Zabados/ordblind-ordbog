Danish Pronunciation

Danish spelling does not reliably predict pronunciation. Silent letters, vowel shifts, and the glot­tal stop (stød) make Danish harder to read aloud than it looks. Each entry gives two layers of help:

ALL-CAPS — a plain-English read-aloud approximation.   /IPA/ — precise phonetic transcription.

The Stød (glottal stop)

Stød is a brief catch or creak in the throat — like the pause in “uh–oh”. In IPA it is written  ˀ  after the affected sound: hund /hunˀ/ (dog), se /seˀ/ (see). The phonetic ALL-CAPS codes do not mark stød — just be aware it exists.

Soft-D

When d appears between vowels or after a vowel at the end of a word, it softens to a fricative — like the th in “the”. Examples: bade (bathe), ved (at/by), sæde (seat). The code writes this as –TH–.

The Danish R

Danish r is uvular — produced at the very back of the throat, like French or German r. After a vowel it often weakens to a schwa /ɐ/ or disappears entirely. The ALL-CAPS codes mostly omit final r sounds as they are inaudible to English ears.

Reading the phonetic codes

The green ALL-CAPS codes use English letter combinations: AH (father), AY (day), EE (feet), OH (go), OO (food), EW (French tu), UR (British fur), AW (law). They are approximations only — always check the IPA for precise pronunciation.
